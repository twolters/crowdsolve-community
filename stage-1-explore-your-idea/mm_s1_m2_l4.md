# Evaluation Instructions

Evaluate the founder’s hypothesis for:

---

## 1. Customer Specificity

Can you clearly picture:
👉 who the hypothesis applies to?

Strong customer definitions:

* are narrow
* are observable
* imply reachability

Weak customer definitions:

* “small businesses”
* “busy professionals”
* “everyone”

---

## 2. Problem Clarity

Does the hypothesis:
👉 clearly identify the customer pain?

Strong problems:

* describe real operational or emotional pain
* connect to earlier lessons
* feel believable

Weak problems:

* are generic
* are abstract
* drift away from prior work

---

## 3. Desired Outcome Strength

Is the outcome:
👉 meaningful and measurable?

Strong outcomes:

* reduce time
* reduce cost
* reduce risk
* improve reliability
* improve confidence
* increase speed

Weak outcomes:

* vague improvement claims
* generic efficiency language
* difficult-to-observe outcomes

---

## 4. Core Solution Mechanism

Can you clearly understand:
👉 how the solution creates the outcome?

Strong mechanisms:

* are simple
* imply behavior change
* connect directly to the problem

Weak mechanisms:

* are feature-heavy
* are overly broad
* sound like technical architecture

---

## 5. Testability

Could this hypothesis:
👉 realistically be tested through interviews, experiments, or an MVP?

Strong hypotheses:

* contain assumptions that can be validated
* imply measurable learning

Weak hypotheses:

* are too broad
* are impossible to test
* rely on undefined concepts

---

## 6. Overall Coherence

Does the hypothesis:
👉 logically connect customer → problem → outcome → mechanism?

Look for:

* alignment
* clarity
* simplicity
* realism

# Output Format

---

# Lesson 4 Feedback — The Hypothesis Formula

---

## 👍 What’s Working

Identify:

* strong specificity
* clear customer targeting
* meaningful outcomes
* strong mechanism clarity
* good testability

(2–4 concise bullets)

---

## ⚠️ Where to Improve

Identify:

* vague customer definitions
* weak outcomes
* unclear mechanisms
* overcomplicated language
* weak testability
* hypothesis drift from earlier lessons

Keep feedback practical and grounded.

---

## 🔧 Suggested Improvements

Provide:

* cleaner wording
* tighter customer framing
* stronger outcome language
* clearer mechanisms
* more testable structure

Preserve the founder’s core thinking.

Do NOT invent a different startup idea.

---

## 🧩 Refined Solution Hypothesis

Provide a tightened and improved version of the founder’s hypothesis using the required structure:

> We believe that
> [specific customer]
> who struggles with [validated problem]
> will achieve [desired outcome]
> if we provide [core solution mechanism].

The refined version should:

* feel specific
* sound realistic
* remain concise
* clearly imply a testable assumption

---

## ⭐ Hypothesis Strength Score (1–5)

Evaluate the overall quality of the hypothesis.

### Scoring Rubric

⭐ 1 — Very vague and difficult to test
⭐ 2 — Some useful thinking, but weak specificity or alignment
⭐ 3 — Clear enough to begin testing
⭐ 4 — Strong, focused, and realistically testable
⭐ 5 — Extremely clear, grounded, and highly testable

---

## 📊 Score Explanation

Explain:
👉 why the hypothesis received this score

Focus on:

* specificity
* testability
* clarity
* alignment
* realism

---

## 💡 Coaching Insight

Provide 1–2 startup principles.

Examples:

* “A startup hypothesis is valuable because it can be proven wrong.”
* “The goal is not certainty—it’s creating a focused learning framework.”
* “Strong hypotheses make it easier to design interviews, experiments, and MVPs.”

---

## 🧠 Preparation for the Next Lesson

Briefly explain:

> “In the next lesson, we’ll identify the hidden assumptions inside your hypothesis and determine which assumptions are the riskiest to test first.”

---

## 🔄 Iteration Prompt

End with:

> “Would you like to refine your hypothesis further, or move on to the next lesson?”
